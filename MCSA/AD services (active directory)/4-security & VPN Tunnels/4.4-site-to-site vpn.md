You have two different networks in two locations:

- **LAN1** → connected to **Router R1** (location: Zagazig)
    
- **LAN2** → connected to **Router R2** (location: Cairo)
A **Leased Line** is a **dedicated physical connection** between two locations provided by an ISP.

leased line : 
- Private line only for your company
    
- Very stable
    
- Very secure
    
- Very expensive

# HOW IT WORKS ? 
LAN1 → R1 → (Dedicated ISP line) → R2 → LAN2 .
Traffic never goes through the public internet.

# connected two location by  encrypted tunnel (VPN): 

Instead of paying for a dedicated line, you use the **public internet** but create an **encrypted tunnel** between the routers.
This is called:

**Site-to-Site VPN**  ,, 
# HOW IT WORKS ? 
vpn though the packets travel through the internet, **encryption makes them secure**.

### Advantages

- Much cheaper
    
- Flexible
    
- Easy to deploy

# ipsec uses two protocols : 
1-**AH (Authentication Header)**  
provides --> authentication , integrity , no encryption .
is uses --> hash function , HMAC .
The data is **not hidden**, but it is **verified**.


2-ESP (Encapsulating Security Payload) 
provides --> encryption , authentication , integrity .
It is the **most used IPsec protocol**.
ESP protects:

- Confidentiality (encryption)
    
- Integrity (Uses **Hashing** to ensure the data wasn't tampered with during transit).
    
- Authentication
- anti-relay   --> Prevents an attacker from intercepting a valid packet and "replaying" it later to trick the system .

So it is used in:

- **VPN tunnels**
    
- **IPsec site-to-site VPN**
    
- **Remote access VPN**




# symmetric encryption (same key for both)  :
- **DES:** Old and insecure (56-bit key).
    
- **3DES:** Better than DES but slow and largely phased out.
    
- **AES:** The modern standard. It comes in 128, 192, or 256-bit strengths. **AES-256** is recommended for high security.

#  Asymmetric Encryption (Public/Private key pair)
- **RSA:** Often used for digital signatures and certificates.
    
- **Diffie-Hellman (DH):** Crucial for VPNs. It allows two parties to establish a shared secret key over an insecure channel. Higher DH groups (like Group 14 or 24) are more secure than lower ones (like Group 2 or 5).
# Integrity & Authentication
How do we know the data is "real" and from the right person?

- **Integrity (Hashing):**
    
    - **MD5:** Fast but considered "broken" for high-security needs.
        
    - **SHA (Secure Hash Algorithm):** Use **SHA-2** (like SHA-256) instead of SHA-1 for better security
- **Authentication Methods:**

- **PSK (Pre-Shared Key):** A simple password shared by both sides.
    
- **PKI (Public Key Infrastructure):** Uses digital certificates issued by a Certificate Authority (CA).

# site to site vpn connecting to AD environment .
![[../../../attachments/Pasted image 20260311094317.png|Pasted image 20260311094317.png]]
- **WAN Side (11.0.0.0/24):** This represents the "untrusted" or public side of your lab. The client on the right is likely assigned an IP like `11.0.0.x`.
    
- **The VPN Gateway:** This is the "Entry Point." It has two legs:
    
    - **WAN IP:** `11.0.0.1` (The destination for your VPN client/tunnel).
        
    - **LAN IP:** `192.168.1.200` (The internal gateway).
        
- **LAN Side (192.168.1.0/24):** This is your "trusted" zone where your Windows Server (AD) lives.


1- the client must successfully negotiate the encryption (AES), integrity (SHA), and authentication (PSK/Cert) with the `.1` interface.
    
2- **Traffic Selectors (Proxy IDs):** The VPN must be told that traffic from the `11.0.0.0/24` network is allowed to reach the `192.168.1.0/24` network.
    
3- **Routing:** The AD server needs to know how to send traffic back. If the AD server's default gateway isn't the VPN device, you’ll need a **Static Route** on the AD server or the core switch pointing back to `.200`.
